// motivation

Why This Problem Matters

Accurate real-time knee angle prediction is essential for controlling prosthetics, exoskeletons, and rehabilitation robots — without requiring bulky external sensors or motion capture labs.

🦿

Exoskeleton Control

Lower-limb exoskeletons need to anticipate knee movement 10ms ahead to provide seamless assistance. Traditional methods require camera-based motion capture, which is impractical outside labs.

Zero external sensors
🧠

EMG-Driven Prediction

Surface electromyography (sEMG) captures muscle activation signals from 4 muscles — Biceps Femoris, Rectus Femoris, Semitendinosus, and Vastus Medialis — microseconds before movement occurs.

4-channel sEMG
🔄

Transfer Learning Challenge

Training a model for each new patient (especially those with knee pathology) is infeasible. We pre-train on 22 healthy subjects and fine-tune on just 3–7 gait cycles of new patient data.

Few-shot adaptation
📊

Novel: Prediction Intervals

Standard models output a single angle. Our novel contribution: Tube Loss produces a calibrated prediction interval [µ₁, µ₂] with guaranteed 90% coverage — critical for clinical safety.

Tube Loss (Anand et al. 2024)

// transfer pipeline

3-Stage Transfer Protocol

01

Pre-training on Georgia Tech

Train CNN-LSTM on 425,093 windows from 21 healthy subjects (AB06–AB28). Leave AB30 for zero-shot test. Loss converges from 682 deg² to 0.36 z² with z-score normalization.

02

Stage-1: Population Adaptation (UCI)

Leave-one-out across 11 training subjects. Per-subject 80/20 split prevents val divergence. Differential LR: CNN layers get 1e-4, LSTM+head get 5e-4–1e-3 based on data volume.

03

Stage-2: Subject Fine-Tuning

50/50 split on test subject data. First half fine-tunes (CNN frozen), second half evaluates. Conformal calibration post-hoc guarantees exact 90% PI coverage without retraining.

// novel contribution

Tube Loss — Prediction Intervals

Instead of predicting a single angle, our model outputs a calibrated interval [µ₁, µ₂] using Tube Loss (Anand et al. 2024). Clinically safe because uncertainty is made explicit.

1

Dual Output Head

The Dense layer outputs 2 neurons per time step: µ₁ (lower bound) and µ₂ (upper bound). The midpoint (µ₁+µ₂)/2 is the point prediction.

2

Asymmetric Loss Function

Points inside the interval are penalized lightly. Points outside are penalized proportionally to how far they fall outside — creating a "tube" of acceptable predictions.

3

Conformal Calibration

After training, we compute nonconformity scores on the fine-tune set and expand intervals by q̂ to guarantee ≥90% empirical coverage (PICP ≥ 0.9) with zero model retraining.

4

Key Metrics

PICP — prediction interval coverage probability (target: 0.90). NMPIW — normalized mean prediction interval width (want smaller). NMAE — error on the midpoint.

5-Step EMG Preprocessing Pipeline

①

High-Pass Filter

20 Hz cutoff. Removes motion artifacts and baseline drift (0–10 Hz).

②

Rectification

Full-wave: |signal|. Converts biphasic EMG to positive activation envelope.

③

Low-Pass Filter

4 Hz cutoff. Smooths envelope to extract muscle activation profile.

④

Downsample

1000 Hz → 100 Hz (stride=10). Well above Nyquist for 4 Hz signal.

⑤

Z-Score Normalize

Per-window (mean=0, std=1). Removes subject amplitude differences.
